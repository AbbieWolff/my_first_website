Video Games

Simulation Games

Young Love

I've been playing PC games since I was in elementary school, if 4th Grade Adventures and SimTown count. I even loved learning to type with Mavis Beacon, playing typing games in class and at home. Yes, I'm a geek--I know.

It was a fateful day in junior high school when a friend introduced me to The Sims. That's when my gaming really started. I could make my own characters, write stories for them, create relationships and design and manage every facet of their lives.

I felt inspired to create complicated soap operas with my simulated characters (Sims). Sometimes, I burnt their houses down, or, in later games, trapped them in pools without ladders, just to watch the mayhem unfold. But, mostly, I tried to create something resembling a "happy" life whenever possible, for every Sim.

The Sims (1) was only the beginning for me. As more expansions to and sequels of the game were released, and as the video game development and general computer technology exponentially improved, my love for games expanded in turn.

Cities & Towns

Age of Empires II was, and still is, one of my most cherished simulation games. I still play it, years later, with fondness, despite its age (pun intended). It's a medieval civilization simulation game. The goal is to direct the cultural development and labor and resource management of small medieval towns. Its successor, Age of Empires III and similar games like Banished, are still my go-to games for engaging (yet somewhat mindless) relaxation and escape.

Similarly, Electronic Arts, the same company that made The Sims games and SimTown, created a modern city simulation series: SimCity.

Some of the SimCity games offer the opportunity to expand your city to include millions of simulated people--if you don't go bankrupt or have a major catastrophe like an earthquake first!

Anno 2070 is a futuristic city simulation that includes more challenging, social gameplay (with other human beings, no less!) and a more complex resource management system.


Role-Playing Games

During college, I finally took the plunge and tried playing video games that don't involve simulated, sandbox storytelling (of people or towns/cities).

Bioware

I fell in love with Bioware at first sight. They tell much better stories than I could on my own, without removing the creativity and power of choice. Bioware games in the Mass Effect and Dragon Age series' tell guided stories with options for dialogue and plot choices that have consequences.

The Mass Effect series tells the tale of Commander Shephard, a majestic hero on an epic science fiction adventure with his/her diverse companions as they try to save the universe from itself, and from complete and total destruction--with mixed results.

The Dragon Age series explores the medieval world of Thedas with a similarly lovable cast of diverse characters, led by three distinct heroes (one in each game) with varied approaches to resolving civil war, religious persecution, magic, and other exciting conflicts.

These role-playing games (RPGs) are story-, character- and culture-focused, with relationship-building (romance and friendship) and world-building (peace and diplomacy) at their core. They are truly excellent games, especially the most recent iterations in each series: Mass Effect 3 and Dragon Age: Inquisition. (See the list of my favorite games below for links to the current Dragon Age and Mass Effect websites.)


New Horizons

Bioware opened up so many more options for me as a gamer. After playing through some of their games, for the first time, I felt capable of playing games that I had previously associated with "hardcore" (usually male) gamers. I felt capable of handling the controls, combat and strategy required to play games like Skyrim, L.A. Noire, Assassin's Creed 3, and even console games by Quantic Dream.

Fear of the Unknown

Before playing Mass Effect, I had been very intimidated by RPGs and any other games that were not simulation games, especially if they were not available on PC and required a console. They felt foreign, unfamiliar, and, most importantly (at the time) meant for boys--not for me, a girl.

Despite being surrounded by a family of gamers (my dad and brothers, but not my mom) and being in a long-term relationship with a self-professed hardcore gamer, I felt ostracized from the gaming community for most of my life. It took years for me to move past these mental and social barriers, to accept and then proudly pronounce myself as a female gamer.

Free to Play

It was ultimately liberating and empowering for me to play through and complete Bioware games, to play as a female character, to form meaningful relationships in-game, and to feel emotionally engaged--instead of disengaged, as I had felt when trying to play many other games.

I can't wait to continue to expand my horizons with a variety of new games (The Witcher 3 and the next Mass Effect game, among others).

I love simulation games and RPGs.

Hopefully, I will eventually have the opportunity to help make games that offer girls and women like me, and the LGBT community I am an ally of, feel represented in games.
